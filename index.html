Do cleaner brands actually make more money?

This mini-dashboard looks at 24 fashion + beauty brands from Chanel to Shein. For each, we estimated a Minimalism score (1–5) based on site and brand aesthetics, then paired it with annual revenue in 2015, 2020, and 2025.

How this dataset works

Each brand is tagged with a segment (ultra-luxury, luxury, premium, mass premium, fast fashion, beauty retail) and a Minimalism score from 1 (maximal, noisy, crowded) to 5 (hyper-minimal, lots of white space, very reduced UI).

We combine that with reported or estimated annual revenue in billions for 2015, 2020, and 2025. The goal isn’t to be perfectly econometric; it’s to give a visual sense of how minimalism and money travel together across the fashion spectrum.

What to look for

  • Do ultra-minimal brands (like Celine) sit in a different revenue band than maximal ones (like Shein)?
  • How do fast fashion giants compare to heritage luxury houses?
  • Which brands grow the most from 2015 → 2025 on the line chart when you change the dropdown?

So… does minimalism “win”?

Not exactly. The cleanest sites (Celine, Hermès, Chanel) tend to live in ultra-luxury, where revenue is high but volume is low. Meanwhile, maximalist platforms like Shein and H&M are less minimal but move insane product at scale.

Minimalism seems to correlate more with positioning (luxury, premium, “quiet” brands) than with pure revenue. What the charts really show is that there’s no single aesthetic path to growth—but the visual language of a brand still sends a strong signal about who it wants as a customer.

Reading notes

Numbers are simplified / smoothed to make patterns readable, not to predict stock prices. Think of this as a visual essay about taste, money, and the way fashion brands choreograph attention on-screen.
